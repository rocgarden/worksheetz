Gardens of Hope: Helping Monarch Butterflies

(1) Carter Steadman didn’t ask for birthday presents.(2) Instead, he asked friends and family to donate toward something he cared about: a small garden for monarch butterflies.
(3) With his parents’ help, he cleared a sunny patch of yard and planted milkweed and other flowers. (4) Milkweed matters because monarch caterpillars depend on it for food.
(5) Carter called the garden a “way station”—a safe place where monarchs could stop, eat, and lay eggs during their long journey.
(6) At first, he waited and watched.(7) Then he began noticing butterflies fluttering in and out of the plants.
(8) Over time, he learned to look closely for tiny eggs under leaves and for striped caterpillars munching their way along.
(9) Carter didn’t just enjoy seeing monarchs. (10) He wanted to help them survive.

Monarch Migration Challenges

(11) Every year, monarchs in North America travel thousands of miles. (12) In the fall, many fly south to mountain forests in Mexico, where they gather together in trees to rest through winter.
(13) When spring arrives, they start north again. (14) The trip takes several generations: adult butterflies lay eggs, those adults die, and the next generation continues the journey.
(15) But in recent years, fewer monarchs have made it. (16) One reason is that milkweed has disappeared in many places as farms and lawns expand. (17) Another reason is that storms and habitat loss can damage the forests where monarchs spend the winter.
(18) That’s why scientists rely on “citizen helpers”—people who track monarchs, tag them, and report sightings. (19) These reports help researchers understand where monarchs are thriving and where they are struggling.
(20) Carter joined this effort by raising and tagging monarchs and by planting more flowers each year. (21) His garden may be small, but it’s part of a much bigger solution: if people protect habitats along the route, monarchs have a better chance to rebound.
